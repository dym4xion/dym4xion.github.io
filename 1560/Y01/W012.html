W012: Peter Sellers' Inspector Clouseau

Week starting: 2020-12-07

The Pink Panther (1963)

Entertaining enough and worth seeing if only for it's cultural significance; mild slapstick and physical comedy; the classic theme tune; and of course, Sellers' performance. What struck me the most was how little 'detectorising' actually takes place and the ending is oddly unsatisfying by modern story-telling standards (it is a farce so playing against genre conventions is to be excpected and to this extent it was interesting to see).

A Shot in the Dark (1964)

A Shot in the Dark hits you with a great opening theme as we witness the central crime plays out using an interesting sort of style that lets us look through the windows of apartment rooms in a sort of Rear Window fashion before the title credits play.

It continues the same mild wit and the bumbling and stumbling Clouseau from the first film but with more of the conventional trappings of a detective story (interviewing witnesses, assembling all of the suspects in one room to finally reveal the murderer).

It's interesting to note that it came out in the same year as Dr Strangelove so if you are only interested in seeing one 1964 Peter Sellers film then maybe shouldn't be this one.

The Return of the Pink Panther (1975)

Released eleven years after A Shot in the Dark (there was an interim Panther film made without Edwards or Sellers), Return sees Clouseau again looking to solve the theft of the Pink Panther diamond by the notorious Phantom. The story-telling gets a bit long-in-the-tooth here. It's hard to really care about the solving of the mystery, but this film does has some great comedic set-pieces which make it at least fleetingly enjoyable. The highlights of these set-pieces would have to be Insp. Clouseau's introduction and his fights with his karate student: Cato.

Notes and Conclusions

Does Clouseau, as a character, live up to Sellers' characters in Kubrick's Lolita or Dr Strangelove? Maybe not, but it is still a fun character who Sellers injects a lot of energy into in each of the films. Mancini's music and Edward's direction are also both strong throughtout, too.

There are only two other Pink Panther films that fit the criteria for watching (Revenge and Strikes Again) so they will have to probably have to be paired with another Sellers or Edwards production to be included in a future week's viewing. The Party (1968) seems a good contender as it was both directed by Blake Edwards and stars Peter Sellers.
