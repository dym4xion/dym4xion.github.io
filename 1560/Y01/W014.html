W014: Tarkovsky

Week starting: 2020-12-21

Ivan's Childhood (1962)

Ivan's Childhood would be a lot harder to watch if it wasn't so visually striking. While it's a gloomy situation our characters find themselves in, the drawn out meditative shots of flooded forests or derelict buildings do embody a sense of contemplative serentity that can let you forget that a war is going on. Not in the background, but not always in the forground either; it's the effects of the war that Tarkovsky is more interested in here, rather than the war in-and-of-itself.

The depictions of battlefield horrors faced by children (or in this case a child) on the Eastern front is a theme it shares with the later Belarusian film Come and See (1985) known widely for it's intense portrayal of the subject. Insomuch as they can, or should, be compared I think that Ivan's Childhood is more reserved and stylistcally superior but I think the two films are trying to do two very different things - I only really use it as a comparator as it's the closest thing I've seen with such obvious similarities.

Much of its visual successes can likely be credited to the film's director of photography Vadim Yusov who really leverages the qualities of the 1.33 aspect ratio and is able to both get up close in small sets as well as capture the height and airy-ness of more expansive landscapes. It's worth seeing on as big a screen in as high a quality as you can! (this is probably true for each of this weeks films).

Concise, melancholic, picturesque and well worth your time, but don't take my word for it.

Andrei Rublev (1966)

From only seeing Solaris and now Ivan's Childhood I would not have expected Tarkovsky to have made a historical epic that can compete with some of the greatest of the historical epic tradition; but in Andrei Rublev he pulls it off to an excellent degree without losing any of the cerebral niceties that I have come to expect.

Billed as a biographical film, it's reasons for being so great is that it precisely isn't! While Andrei Rublev is the central character in the story, more often than not he is sidelined by the more significant events that take place around him (a Tartar invasion, the casting of a bell). The end result, then, is a gorgeuosly painted look at medieval Russian life in the Tarkovsky style - and it's because of it's long run time, not in spite of it, that it can be a really sublime piece of historical filmmaking with great compassionate and humanising streaks.

We are once again graced with both Yusov's great cinematography and Nikolay Burlyaev's (Ivan from Ivan's Childhood) acting as the bell maker. It's interesting to note the differences and similarities across the two parts of Andrei Rublev. For this reason (and for the film's runtime) it's worth making use of the intermission.

"Everything is vanity and decay."

Stalker (1979)

The less you know about this film the better, so if you have even the slightest interest in this film you should see it before reading anything else.

Leaveraging sepia and colour photography to tell a story taking place in two distinct worlds, Stalker is both unnerving and hard to take your eyes off of. With it's mind-bending unrealities and tranquil-yet-disturbed meditations on nature, Stalker's most obvious comparitor to me (and I'm sure to many others) seems to be 2018's Annihilation. The differences between the two are clear but what is fascinating about the similarities are that how what can be found unsettling in the human experience can be constant across even the most significant lines of separation. Stalker and Annihilation exist in two quite different social, political, and geographic landscapes and yet the reasons why both are so unsettling are the same. They both invoke similar kinds of fear of the unknown; self-destructive tendencies; the human relationship to their environment; the idea of profoundly localised yet universal experiences; having seemingly concrete conceptions of the world peverted; and crossing threasholds from which there is no return. For a more robust analysis, check out this clip from The Pervert's Guide to Cinema.

The music by Edward Artemiev is just as beautiful here as in Solaris and I'm looking forward to the watching The Mirror (1974) to be able to hear more of on film.

Notes and Conclusions

Having already seen Solaris I was already eager to see more Tarkovsky, and I'm surprised it was taken this long. Regardless, I am glad that I did see these three, each brilliant in their own right but work together well if you are trying to get a sense of Tarkovsky's work.
