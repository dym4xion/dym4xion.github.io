W011: Chantal Akerman

Week starting: 2020-11-30

Je Tu Il Elle (1974)

Maybe the most simple of the three, Je Tu Il Elle does a lot with very little. Akerman proves here to be very competent at acting as well as writing/directing. It goes without saying that it is ambitious for its time, or even by today's standards, as such it's not for the prudish.

Jeanne Dielman, 23, quai du commerce, 1080 Bruxelles (1975)

Definitely unlike anything I have seen, Jeanne Dielman does away with complex story or conventional framing in exchange for bucket-loads of subtlety and interesting cinematography to portray three days in the life of it's main character. If you want a nice synopsis, who better to give one than Akerman herself.

Time is important in this film. The three days roughly equally break up the three hour runtime but we only see the second half and first half of the first and third days (respectively) so why is the second day compressed? Maybe it means something, maybe it doesn't, but Ivone Margulies gives an interesting analysis of the film over here.

Notes and Conclusions

In their reserved styles these films force us to ask questions about what is going on. Questions for which there may be no answers. Who is the Tu in Je Tu Il Elle? Why do the potatoes get overcooked on the second day of Jeanne Dielman? What does Anna want in Les Rendez-vous d'Anna?

Some further commonalities between these films include a great sensibility for set dressing, especially in Jeanne Dielman. I would love to be able to walk around that set to get a closer look at the all the small things that bring that appartment to life. A further commonality is the introspective/autobiographical quality. When we see Jeanne or Anna's loneliness or lacking sense of belonging it would seem to be Akerman's own feelings we see too.

As with any film that reaches to do something experimental, the more you 'go with' these films the more you will 'get out' of them. I found Les Rendez-vous d'Anna to be the most 'accessible' of the three, by which I mean that it's probably the least experimental, or even the least ambitious, but I think it's the most deftly presented and the most enjoyable under conventional standards.
