W007: Youssef Chahine

Week starting: 2020-11-02

Cairo Station (1958)

You could say that Cairo Station is a bit like City Lights meets Brief Encounter meets Psycho, but I think that would be doing the film a bit of a disservice. It's fast-paced and chaotic and you feel as the film will run away from you if you don't keep up with it. It's also really quite amazing how many things this film touches on in such a short run time. In only 77 minutes it looks at: copycat crime, labour unions, sexuality, religion, class, poverty, insanity, fatherhood, musicals, marriage, obsession, women's rights; and not with a lost to depth, as some of these themes intersect and interrelate in a way that this film captures in an interesting way.

Saladin (1963)

This long epic concerning the Christian crusades, told moreso from the Arab perspective, revels in its strategic, political, and religious complexities. It is the complexity and nuance that is maybe what makes it most interesting as you follow the developing romance between the Christian crusader, Louisa, and the Catholic Arab, Issa, repeatedly separated and brought together by conflict. It's geopolitics are still relevant today and are a sad reminder of how little can sometimes change even over long periods of time. Maybe this is what makes the best historical epics so good: to highlight the historicity of modern problems.

Alexandria... Why? (1979)

Good autobiographical filmmaking, Alexandria... Why? continues Cairo Station's social realism but this time looking at the experience of Alexandrians during WWII. As a lot of WWII is told from the European, American or Japanese perspective, it was refreshing to see it from the Egyptian point-of-view. The commonalities and differences from what we might be used to in more standard homefront depictions are interesting to observe - the use of interjected war footage compounds this.

Perhaps Alexandria... Why's most commendable merit is, like its leading character, that it knows exactly what it wants. Everything feels purposeful in its aims to celebrate the virtues of ambition, creativity, resilience and love.

Notes and Conclusions

Each of these films are immensely bold for their time; and in a lot of ways they are still bold today. I'm glad I have seen them and I am looking forward to watching more of Chahine's films, a privalige I am afforded by way of many of them (including these three) being on Netflix in the U.K.
