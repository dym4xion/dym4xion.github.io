W016: Larisa Shepitko

Week starting: 2021-01-04

Wings (1966)

Wings is a nice look at the often re-visited topic of military service personnel adapting to civilian life but this time in an environment less often portrayed. Its merits are its understatedness yet social and interpersonal astuteness (the awkward first time meeting between our main character and their son-in-law is feels uncannily real). At its heart it seems to look at the lasting social tensions caused by the war: but at a mostly personal level. Nadya feels astranged from her adopted daughter and as her recollection of her time at war strengthens, it is at the cost of her ability to relate moreso to her peacetime comrades. None of this, however, is without a robust, if reserved, sense of humour and stylisically sound photography.

You and Me (1971)

In You and Me Shepitko continues the sort of calm realist drama she sets out in Wings with a similar sort of dialogue style that does a great job of showing (rather than telling) us what the characters are like. The themes explored in this one are maybe a bit more cerebral than those of Wings, in that it looks more at ephermeral qualities of mental dispositions and tries to capture that using the textural qualities of what can be done on film. For such a diffcult task, the results are quite cogent even if it is a little meandering and hard to follow in the beginning. This is largely rectified by its visual strikingness and competency (the circus scene is a great technical achievement) also it possesses a great sense of charm that is well conveyed in some thoughtfully drawn out scenes.

Ascent (1977)

I find that The Ascent really speaks for itself so I don't really know what of any value I could say about it. It is technically very good and Shepitko does a good job of transferring what was so good about the previous two films (the reservedness, the realism) into a radically different setting. All the brutality of the Russian experience of the war is here, and Shepitko's deftness for portraying cinematically how real people behave only makes it more heartbreaking. It's totally gripping and totally immersive thanks to some really great performances and outdoor locations.

Notes and Conclusions

Nothing really to add. Each are good examples of maybe what could be called 'quiet cinema' as they are very impactful and achieve a lot but without any exaggarated drama or over stylisation. These are some very real feeling stories that are told well and in such a way that it respects it's audience. I'd like to see her other major film: Heat (1962) (her first feature), but I haven't been able to find it anywhere online.
