W010: Ralph Bakshi

Week starting: 2020-11-23

Heavy Traffic (1973)

Heavy Traffic lives upto its Adults Only certification and isn't for the faint of heart. Here we get a cartoonishly brutal and hyperviolent look at Ralph Bakshi's New York City, blending some live action scenery with highly animated characters. At its center is a portrayal of youthful directionlessness and the pains and joys that come with that, as well as exaggerated looks at the interactions between NYC's minority communities.

Wizards (1977)

Super inventive and visually striking, Wizards considers serious topics in a way that is consistently engaging and frequently funny. Maybe it's in parts unsubtle, but it's a cartoon and can afford to be so without being overbearing. The neo-Nazism its villain invokes seems intentionally cartoonish in order to make a bigger point about how visual spectacles can be used to drive hysteria and fear to be wielded to cause large-scale death and destruction. Interesting blending of fantasy and modern technology.

The Lord of the Rings (1978)

I don't know if it can be said that Bakshi's TLotR is better than Peter Jackson's adaptation, but it's defintely cooler and heaps more charming. It's an incopmlete story and finsishes around about after the battle at Helm's Deep, but that doesn't mean you don't get a high dose of really expressive and human-looking rotoscope animation, along with some great voice work and really cool background artwork. The music is well suited, if overly conventional (I read on Wikipedia that Bakshi wanted to use some Led Zeppelin as part of the soundtrack, which would have been fun to see). It would be cool if a second part ever got made.

Notes and Conclusions

I enjoyed these films a lot and as a trilogy I think they provide a decent look at some of the highlights of Bakshi's career. In my mind, The Lord of the Rings is the most technically refined and functions best as a conventional film (even if the story is incomplete) but Wizards and Heavy Traffic are conceptually brilliant and shouldn't be missed if given the opportunity to see them.

If not restricted by the 1980 inclusive limit then it may have been a good idea to do RB's fantasy films one week (Wizards, The Lord of the Rings, Fire and Ice (1983)), and his films set in NYC another week (Fritz the Cat (1977), Heavy Traffic, Coonskin (1975)). I'd definitely like to see some more of his other films if and when I find a thematic triple to put it in them.
