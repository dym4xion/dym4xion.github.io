W018: Starring Sissy Spacek

Week starting: 2021-01-21

Badlands (1973)

Based loosely on real events Badlands' only real sin is a certain amount of predictability (having already seen two Malick films); but maybe this is the wrong attitude to have with this being Malick's first. You'll be getting all the cool sights and sounds and nature that seem to be Malick's staples, but the writing in Badlands feels quite a bit sharper than in Days of Heaven (1978) or A Hidden Life (2019), but conversely I think the latter two have better photography. Spacek (in what seems to become a pattern) has great chemistry with Sheen and are both very strong in their portrayals of some interesting characters. Her v/o work is especially fun much in the same way of Manz's in Days of Heaven.

3 Women (1977)

I don't think it has quite fully sank in for me how great 3 Women is. It's greatest achievement, in my mind, is how weird it gets while remaining fully in the material realm. It doesn't force you to buy into any alternate dimension woo space magic, and yet it is still totally bizarre. The music, I hazard, is doing a lot of the legwork in this regard as the cast's performances are quite reserved (this works to the film's benefit). Spacek and Duvall are brilliant together and what we see of Rule is great too. The film provides interesting observations on personality and dreams in a package that is equal parts saccharine and uneasy.

Coal Miner's Daughter (1980)

Coal Miner's Daughter tells the story of country singer Loretta Lynn's rise to fame. Spacek and Jones have generally good on-screen chemistry and Spacek does a particularly good job of covering Lynn's songs. A gimmick I always apreciate is an actor having to learn a particularly distinct skill for a role: examples being The Matrix cast learning martial arts or Ralph Feinnes learning ballet and Russian for The White Crow (2018). Spacek is more than competent in this regard and the film's soundtrack is definitely worth checking out.

What I can't make heads or tails of is the inclusion of a quite blatant marital rape scene. Is it part of a commitment to an honest portrayal of events? Is it 1980s shorthand for 'a troubled relationship that is still ultimately held together by a genuine love'? One shouldn't forget that marital rape was not made illegal in all the United States until 1993. I have nothing really conclusive to say about this but it stuck in my mind enough to be worth mentioning.

Notes and Conclusions

Spacek is as good in each of these as she is in Carrie (1976) and as I haven't seen any of her other films I have no idea whether these are even her best but they are a good amount of fun regardless.
