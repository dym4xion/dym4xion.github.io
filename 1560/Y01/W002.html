W002: The Dollars Trilogy

Week starting: 2020-09-28

A Fistful of Dollars (1964)

I think what I like most about A Fistful of Dollars (AFOD) is how simple it is. You can easily see where the plot is going early on in the film, but that doesn't detract too much from the ability to enjoy the it. Instead of a heady plot or high concept ideas, what AFOD offers is a series of fun set pieces, set to great music.

It makes sense that the plot of the film isn't its proudest achievement, given that it rips off the plot of Akira Kurosawa's Yojimbo (1961) (see next week). However, that isn't to say that AFOD's other components are not an artistic achievement in themself. Leone establishes here a style here that is carried out and developed throughout the rest of this week's films and, in turn, will go on to influence many other films. This style isn't appreciated by anyone moreso than Quentin Tarantino who really encorporates a lot of Leone into his own films as he describes in this article in the Spectator.

For a Few Dollars More (1965)

For a Few Dollars More (FAFDM) sees Eastwood and Volonte returning as the same totally different characters, but this time they are joined by Lee Van Cleef's Colonel Mortimer, who does a great job of introducing a third perspective to the film of a competing-bounty-hunter-turned-accomplice to Eastwood's Man with No Name. The film develops what AFOD established in a really interesting way that leaves you satisfied by the end. Even though I think it's the best of the three films (see notes), I don't have much to add other than it's a lot of fun to watch and everything that was great about the previous film returns here in full force.

The Good, the Bad and the Ugly (1966)

The Good, the Bad and the Ugly (GBU) is a much bigger film than the two that preceed it - by almost every measure. Despite being around an hour longer than AFOD, GBU is still as ennjoyable, and rarely feels as if it is dragging. Even some of the more minute scenes that don't contribute much to the plot are enjoyable to watch. I think this must be due to how lightweight the central plot is. It really can be summarised as "go to this place to find the hidden gold". As such, you only need to care about the plot to the extent that it lets you experience the antics of the three main characters. The American Civil War features quite significantly in the film and the subject is handled in a surprisingly deft way.

Something that occurred to me early on in the film was that there are probably quite a few people who have seen GBU without first seeing the other two, due to this film's greater success. I wonder how much this affects how much you enjoy the film as I remember several years ago trying, and failing, to sit through all of GBU but this time I was much more up for it and it didn't feel as much of a drag as I remember from my first attempt watching it.

Notes and Conclusions

One of these film's most distinguishing characteristics is Ennio Morricone's score. I don't know enough about music to say exactly why the score is so good, but what I think is most special about the score is how directly it is tied in with what we are seeing on screen. This audio/visual harmony, I think, is what allows these films to be 'meaningful' without needing to say much. Instead of long monologues or other devices that may be used to direct the viewer's attention to a specific point the film is making, the way music and videography is combined expresses something far more ephemeral but no less human or fun to watch.

For my money, FAFDM is the best of the three and probably works best as a stand alone film. I'm not certain I would have enjoyed AFOD as much if I didn't know there was more of the same to come. Equally, I don't think I would have enjoyed GBU without first seeing it's predecessors which engaged me with, and made me hungrier for Leone's filmmaking. FAFDM, however, stands out for me as a film that strikes the best balance for what these films are trying to do, whereas GBU (which is generally taken to be the pinacle of these films) is sometimes too meandering and AFOD is ultimately less impactful. Justin Andress on inverse.com gives a more comprenesive write up of why it is the better film, so read that for more on this point.

Finally, I wanted to include an interesting point made by YouTube commenter John Maddin made under the a clip of the final scene of FAFDM: "I always found it funny that these movies were bankrolled by German money, shot in Spain, directed by an Italian with mostly Italian actors who spoke next to no English with just the one or two American actors and these spaghetti movies were the first to depict a more realistic and believable Old West, before this an American western starred John Wayne or Randolph Scott and were totally devoid of realism and the dirt, hardship and the general nitty gritty of the wild west. The spaghetti westerns were the first that felt more believable".
