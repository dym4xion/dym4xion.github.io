W009: Agnès Varda

Week starting: 2020-11-16

La Pointe Courte (1955)

While a lot of La Pointe Courte's realism comes from the way it's shot, what shouldn't go unmentioned is how good its writing is too. Featuring such zingers as "I came here to leave you but there are better things to do", "they talked too much to be happy" and "the bonds betweens us are stronger than we are"; it's this layering of small details (an infestation of cats, obscure local sports, fishing techniques) that encourages you to accept what the film tries to give you.

Cléo from 5 to 7 (1962)

Probably my favourite of the three, Cléo from 5 to 7 is a sharp, intimate and quick-witted look at the narcissism that comes with moderate fame. But that's not all. We also get an insight on medical anxiety, superstition and the subjectivity of percieved time and a lot of cool imagery and camera work all within a consise runtime. So much cool imagery, in-fact, that it was hard to chose a single frame for the banner image.

Le Bonheur (1965)

Maybe Le Bonheur's strongest point is its ambiguity. It looks very good, but I know that it is using how it looks to try to be deceptive. This is proabably one of the harder films to put into words exactly why I enjoyed watching it, but its subtle and non-judgemental commentary that walks the line between cynicism and passion in a saturated Hyper-realist framing makes for really interesting viewing. It will have you asking yourself silly questions like "what does happiness look like?" and "can we be honest with each other? or even ourselves?"

Notes and Conclusions

Varda's skills as a documentarian are present even in these narative films. In La Pointe Courte we are not only witnessing the struggles of a couple's marriage but we also get to see, in documentary fashion, the lives of a fishing community and their own struggles. Despite Cléo from 5 to 7 being a fictional story, it is one told not unlike a documentary, where we have a central figure exposed to some amount of fame, and we see how that affects them and those around them. In a certain way it remined me of Sophie Fiennes' Grace Jones: Bloodlight and Bami (2017); how appropriate then Fiennes also made a doc-short on Lars von Trier titled: Lars from 1-10 (2001).

Colour is important in these films. La Pointe Courte is filmed in black and white (I don't know if this is out of necessity or not - but is works thematically) and reflects certains contrasts of the film's subjects. Cléo from 5 to 7 is almost entirely in black and white except for early shots of a tarot reading (as if anticipating more accessible colour photography technology). Le Bonheur is shot entirely in colour, generally using a extremely vibrant palette, and occasionally transitions using fades to blue, red, green, purple, yellow, and black only once we have reached the end. It's this storytelling through colour (combined with interesting staging and editing techniques and a good sense of humour) that really highlights what I like about Varda and what makes her films so interesting to watch. See this documentary short for a small taste of that.
