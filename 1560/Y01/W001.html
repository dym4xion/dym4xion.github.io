W001: Costa-Gavras

Week starting: 2020-09-21

Z (1969)

Z works well as a film largely through it's ability to take what could otherwise be a fairly simple story of politically motivated assassination and relate it to very human experiences in an engaging way. The film very much lives up to its many acolades. Its punchy editing and music which won Academy and BAFTA awards (respectively) help keep the film alive and its political commentary still rings coherently today despite being over 50 years old.

Retelling the story of the assassination of the Greek politician Grigoris Lambrakis, but this time (for political reasons) set in Algieria, Z does a great job of avoiding straying into the exaggarated or hyperbolic too frequently. Instead it is able to use brief moments of humour or violence when following the murder investigation in a way that more realistically reflects our own world. My favourite scenes would have to be the ones which follow 'The Judge' and his relatively cooly conducted investigation. The way in which the local officials and agitators try their best to wiggle their way out any responsiblity or accountability is astutely depicted and at times nothing short of hilarious.

I really enjoyed how the ending was executed, primarily for how it escapes the trap of ending either too sentimentally or too pessimistically. It ends aptly, if somewhat irreverently, for a film that has an especially authentic perspective on the consequences of systemic corruption.

The Confession (1970)

The Confession continues Gavra's work in poltitical thrillers and while this film may be less fun to watch than Z it is just as impactful in its presentation. Like Z, The Confession adapts a real story, this time the story of Czechoslovak communist politician Artur London and the torture he endured to obtain a coerced confession for a show trial.

What I found most interesting about this film is that, whether intentionally or not, it functions very well as a demonstration of the idea of politics-as-religion. Throughout the film there are several references to having 'faith' in the party; in certain rooms there are portraits of Stalin and Gottwald (functioning as icons); and, most glaringly, the title of the film and its main subject matter point to the act of confession: a component of the Sacrament of Reconciliation in the Catholic Church.

Beyond religious subtext, The Confession is still a great insight into authoritarianism, and really let's us sympathise with its characters.

State of Siege (1973)

State of Siege features great music from Mikis Theodorakis, who also did the music for Z. Rather than assassination or torture, this film looks closely at a group of rebels looking to gain leverage over the US backed Uruguayan government through kidnapping public officials. The film does a great job of expressing the rage felt towards US regime change action in Latin America as well the moral quandaries faced by rebels.

Interestingly, when watching State of Siege I was reminded of a quote at the end of Roger Ebert's review of The Confession where he brings up the point that anti-war activist Daniel Berrigan addresses in an open letter to the Weathermen that "no cause was worth the sacrifice of a single human life". I think this critique of martydom is something that can be seen throughout each of the films seen this week, in that as much as some of the characters may try to achieve their goals, they are ultimately subjected to factors outside of their control.

Notes and Conclusions

These films do not only share a director but also are related through the performance of Yves Montand, who plays central characters in each of the films. He gives a solid performances with his role in The Confession being the most impressive. However, I think it wouldn't be too controversial to say that there is not much range in each of these characters with each of them being a public official of some kind - in such a way that they are fairly interchangeable (aesthetically, not politically).

Moreso, I think these films work well together as they each tap into a real sense of frustration and unfairness, particularly within a political context, that has a universal appeal. Despite grappling with what can be fairly complex subject matters, these films are nonetheless easy to follow and fun to watch.
