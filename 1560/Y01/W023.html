W023: Birds

Week starting: 2021-02-22

The Birds (1963)

Mechanically, The Birds functions in the same way in which all good zombie films do in that there is a nice slow build that provides the background environment which is then corrupted by an extreme cataclysm. Furthering this, it even features the standard zombie film trope whereby the cast of characters, when confronted by the intitial violence, must discuss the best course of action where one voice is a skeptic, the one or two people who actually experienced the attack appear as hysterical, and then perhaps one or two people accept the apparent hysteria has sincere concern. Finally on this, once the main cast has successfully evaded the swarm they end up finding themselves attempting to develop a fortified stronghold anticipating further attacks. Such tropes serve only as a testament to The Birds' influence (but also Vincent Price's Last Man on Earth and, later, the George Romero films) on modern zombie films and television.

Contained in this film is also all of the ususal psychological intrigue you would expect from a Hitchcock thriller like Rear Window (1954) or Vertigo (1958) and in my mind is just as effective as those two, even if less highly celebrated.

Kes (1969)

Dai Bradley's performance is commendable if not only for his acting then his kestrel handling skills too. As such the training scenes with the bird are some of the most enjoyable in the film and are a nice break from the otherwise difficult life faced by Billy and his family.

Jonathan Livingston Seagull (1973)

Did you know that there is no such thing as a Seagull? That is: there is no one specific species that is identified by the name 'Seagull' but that there are several species of gull many of which live by the sea. It is the material qualities of Jonathan Livingston Seagull (like the material qualities of the Common gull or the Great black-backed gull) that set it apart from other films. I don't think JLS has anything that interesting to say, rather the impressions the film left on me, in terms of its sights and sounds, are significant; and in watching it you may end up asking yourself questions like: how did they get all those birds to do that? or: wow doesn't that look beautiful? To this extent, because the story is so ridiculous, I think a better film could be edited using the same material (the great Neil Diamond songs, select pieces of the hushed voice-over dialogue, beautiful gull cinematography) to make a more experimental montage art film expressing the hardships of everday gulls; rather than the strange new-age messianic story it tries to sell. Maybe some sort of fan-edit is warranted.

Notes and Conclusions

Despite it being the least appreciated - or rather: the most hated - JLS has, for some reason, captured my attention more than the other two this week. I think because The Birds and Kes are so obviously good and JLS is so obviously bad (but in a strange way very technically impressive) that falling into the rabbit hole of how and why JLS was made has proved quite interesting. The best source I've found on this is the video 'In Defence of Jonathan Livinston Seagull'.
