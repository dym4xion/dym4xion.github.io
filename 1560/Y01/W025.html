W025: With Music from Joan Baez

Week starting: 2021-03-08

Sacco e Vanzetti (1971)

The last thing you might expect about Sacco e Vanzetti is that it was filmed in Dublin. This must be a testament to transportive quality of the film. They do a really convincing job of placing you in the Italian anarchist/immigrant community of 1920s Boston. It's not as if I can attest to the accuracy of this portrayal but the film never really forces you to suspend any disbelief; you can really buy into the pre-McCarthyist First Red Scare setting.

It makes for enjoyable viewing largely for the same reasons Costa-Gavras' and Lina Wertm√ºller's films do, as it takes obliquely political concepts and can relate them to a completely human perspective. Gian Maria Volonte gives the same stellar performace that he does in the first two Dollars films and his co-stars are strong too.

Baez and Morricone collaborate well together to create some great music for the film. Baez's lyrics and vocals are heartbreaking and Morricone's composition is, as expected, very moving. Thematically, it suits the film very well which is no surprise for an original score by 'the Maestro'. The stand-out final song Here's to You is brilliantly simple with the last two lines for the repeated verse coming from an interview with Vanzetti.

Joe Hill (1971)

We start this section with a slight mystery. The song Joe Hill sung by Joan Baez is not featured in the film as far as I can tell despite being credited on her IMDb page as an uncredited performer of the song and being credited in the VHS home release's end-credits. That the restored version doesn't credit Baez in the opening credits (and has no end-credits) brings me to two possible conclusions: (1) Baez's cover of the song was in the original theatrical release but was cut from home format releases for whatever reason and someone forgot to change the credits, or, (2) Baez was credited in the end-credits of the VHS release, despite the song not being used in the film, as an indication of a significant source of inspiration. This second reason makes a bit more sense in the context that two years prior Baez sang it at Woodstock which became one of the more popular recordings. If you like the song the Paul Robeson and Pete Seegler covers are worth checking out; as is the The Ballad of Joe Hill by Phil Ochs.

In regards to the film itself, I was very impressed. I wanted to put that commonly regarded idea of cinema being a sort of universal language to the test by firstly: tolerating the poor video quality of the copy I had; and secondly: not searching out subtitles for the film (even though I'm sure they are widely avaiable online) so that I had really no idea what was being said during the Swedish speaking parts. This experience really demonstrated the truth to this cinema universalism sentiment because despite the subtractions from a normal film viewing experience, I was still really engaged with the film and it went along nicely. Bo Widerberg's direction is competent yet adventurous and Thommy Berggren has all the energy of a young Pacino.

Silent Running (1972)

I feel fortunate to not have been cursed by watching this film as a child as I'm sure that if I did I would be as overly sentimental about it as Edgar Wright or Mark Kermode, two people I otherwise respect. The film does have some great special FX and set design; all the practical components of the film (music included) work together well but it is let down by a particularly silly story. I don't know why this film is evoking, from me, such a negative response but I can't help but feel that this may perhaps be the best made stupid film I have seen in a long time and could be markedly improved simply by removing all of the dialogue.

Bruce Dern is properly hammy, which is a shame as we never really get much of an insight from the other human characters, so it lacks a bit of the human dimensionality that I think the film wants to celebrate. As such, the three robots are the film's most sympathetic characters and I like to think that when Lowell isn't looking they talk behind his back about how crazy he is for killing his crewmates before even trying to argue his case for saving the biodomes.

There's an amazing disonance of imagination where the film can concieve of interplanetary freighter spaceships with self sustaining biodomes but not a automatic snooker table that can reset all of the balls at a pace faster than one ball every 10 seconds!

Despite raising moral questions I don't think it has any real morality and calling it 'morally ambiguous' would be too much of a complement: it's moreso just morally inconsistent, confused or corrupt. When the film asks you "how far would you go to save the last surviving forest?" I don't think it's too cynical to retort with equally silly questions like "but where are the bears and wolves?" or "what is nature for, when (in the fantasy of the film) all of humanity's needs are satisfied?"

Baez's singing is beautiful regardless, even if sparsely used.

Notes and Conclusions

The films that most warrant comparison are obviously the first two. Both concern the early history of the American labour movement paired in some way with the treatment of early 20th century immigrants. It's odd how strikingly similar they are in that both end up with the main character(s) -- both of which being immigrant labour organisers -- facing an unjust death sentence trial and, albiet more superficially, that both films came out in the same year.
