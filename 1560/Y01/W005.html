W005: Post-War Brit-Crime

Week starting: 2020-10-19

It Always Rains on Sunday (1947)

Less a crime film as much as it is a family drama/slice-of-life providing an interesting insight on how people lived in the few years after the war, complete with: Anderson shelters, rations books, haddock for breakfast, and baths in the kitchen. As pointed out in this BFI clip it's sort of a protogentic soap opera or kitchen sink drama, so it's interesting to watch in that respect.

Interesting analysis here too.

Brighton Rock (1948)

Interwar setting.

The highlight of the film has to be Richard Attenborough's sinister mob youth 'Pinkie' but also great stuff from Hermione Baddeley too.

Theological components.

Surprisingly violent.

The Blue Lamp (1950)

Has more of a focus on the workings of the police: the story following a new copper in a city with "homes broken and demoralised by war".

It's realism can be a bit mundane and it was one of the harder films to get a nice cinematic screenshot for the heading image. Could imagine it having a spin off TV programme; it's quite reserved in its attempt to portray police life in a mostly positive light.

More interesting analysis here than I could give.

Notes and Conclusions

With each of these films clocking in at around 90 minutes, I thought that it would be a good idea to try to watch all of the films back-to-back and see how that would effect the experience. I don't think I will be trying it again; I think it's better to watch one, think about it for a day or two, then watch the next - if only so that I end up having more to say about each film.

I'm not sure I could fully recommend any of these films as films that must be seen in the same way I could recommend Kurosawa's or Lean's films from the past few weeks, but they are entertaining enough and not too long. Which makes me wonder whether I have been spoiling myself in the previous four weeks with highly regarded classics. It stands to reason that across 1560 films made before 1981, that not all of them are going to be earth-shatteringly revelatory and exciting films. Plenty of them will be just OK.
