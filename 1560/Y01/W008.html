W008: Eisenstein

Week starting: 2020-11-09

Alexander Nevsky (1938)

Alexander Nevsky is too modest to be considered a true 'epic' film. It is, however, a really great piece of historical filmmaking.

Featuring some nice music from Sergei Prokofiev; it's interesting to witness here the kind of film scoring that would later become so influential. This music does a lot of leg work in terms of the emotional impact of the film, but more often that not the the music and the photography work hand-in-hand to achieve something completely sublime. The early scenes at Pskov are some of my favourites for how brutal and horrific they are.

Another highlight is the excellent contume design work, with costumes so bold that they practically tell the story on their own.

The 'Battle of the Ice' is, like Prokofiev's score, as iconic as it is influential. We see the components in these scenes which would go on to influence future filmmakers.

Ivan the Terrible, Part 1 (1944)

In the early scenes we're given and insight into the strange religious procedures and rituals involved in a coronation of a Tsar. Which makes me ask: why would the soviet censors allow such a glorification of a monarchic government? I'm sure the answer has something to do with the commonalities of how power functions in both forms leadership: "A state without reins is as uncontrollable as a horse without a bridle." This depiction of power is furthered in Part 2 as Ivan becomes more isolated, paranoid and resigned.

Ivan the Terrible, Part 2 (1945)

Part 2 sees the continuation of Ivan's struggle against the Boyars and it's just as fun to watch. The highlight being the Moiseyev-esque 'Dance of the Oprichniki' - a supurb synthesis of cinema, music, and choreography.

Notes and Conclusions

Moskvin and Tisse's cinematogtaphy is consistently beautiful through each film. Nikolay Cherkasov's performances only get stronger throughout each film (whether as Ivan or Alexander), the peak then being in Ivan 2, which makes it even more a loss that Ivan the Terrible, Part 3 was never finished and released. What remains though is interesting to watch.

I've thoughroughly enjoyed my time with these Eisenstein films. I was tempted this week to watch October instead of Alexander Nevsky as I am still yet to see it despite having already seeing Battleship Potemkin and Strike; but I think the three presented here provide a more consistent thematic trilogy. I anticipate I'll watch October soon enough when I decide on two other films to pair it with.
