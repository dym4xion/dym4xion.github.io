W004: David Lean

Week starting: 2020-10-12

Great Expectations (1946)

Great Expectations checks all of the boxes that you would expect from a good Dickens adaptation. It is very funny throughout and features a litany of zany characters with silly names and is niether too self-serious nor does it shy away from more sentimental gestures. I think you can attribute these same reasons to the success of Armando Iannucci's The History of David Copperfield (2019) but also that both stories share components that give them a universal appeal. This universal/timeless appeal (which probably comes from Dickens moreso than any other component of the film) is maybe best demonstrated by the fact that since 1917 there have been no less that 11 film adaptations of this film.

The spooky foggy scenes in on the marshes and at the graveyard in the beginning of the film really stick in the mind as the most visually stiking parts, but it has really great photography throughout.

The Bridge on the River Kwai (1957)

Lots of great stuff in this one but I don't have much to say about it. It offers some interesting things to think about on morality in war, specifically to do with prisoners of war, but I think it works well mostly because it is fun to watch. Great performances from Alec Guiness but from Sessue Hayakawa as well and strong direction too. Worth seeing if only for the fact that it's a major cultural touch-stone.

Lawrence of Arabia (1962)

We turn to another war epic here but Lawrence is much greater in scope. It runs at about 3 hours and 40 minutes and chronicles the embedding of a British soldier among Arab tribes during the Arab Revolt. It's a good exercise in patient filmmaking/filmwatching as there is a lot of worthwhile things to take away from this film, but it takes its time to deliver them.

It features, again, some great photography but this time of vast desert expanses and also like Bridge some subtle but provocative commentary, still relevant today. My favourite exchange:

SHERIF ALI: "A man can do whatever he wants. You said."
LAWRENCE: "He can ... but he can't want what he wants."

I think that these line encapsulate so much of what this film is about: it says so much without really saying anything at all. The same is true of the very last scene which is devastating despite being so understated. "Everything is here for those willing to look for it".

Notes and Conclusions

David Lean shares with Dickens an intense understanding of humanity and how to effectively translate that in art. For this reason primarily do these films work well together, as they each share this quality. He's a great 'storyteller director' in the sense that at the core of these films is a 'good' story, but Lean's interest with the different roles in making a film (such as editing) ensure that the story is cohesively told.

I was intending to see how these films compare to another Lean film that I have already seen: Brief Encounter, but they are so different that they are not worth comparing. Brief Encounter is great though, as is Zizek's use of it in examining 'The Big Other' in this clip.
