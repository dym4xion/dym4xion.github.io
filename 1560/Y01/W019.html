Week starting: 2021-01-25

Late Spring (1949)

Beautifully ordinary and packed full of a certain kind of charm. Arguably as good as Tokyo Story even if not as widely celebrated.

Tokyo Story (1953)

Isn't life disappointing?

An Autumn Afternoon (1962)

The use of colour is well considered and the urban photography interstitials are strikingly beautiful, too. Obvious plot similarities to Late Spring but with a greater focus on the father and his friendships with his former classmates and teacher - a closer examination of masculinity to this extent.

Notes and Conclusions

A smarter person may have chosen to report on the 'Noriko Trilogy': an informal collection of three Ozu films (two featured this week) where Setsuko Hara plays a character named Noriko but I wasn't aware of this until about halfway through the week and it seemed to me the association between the films is informal enough and non-narrative based that it wouldn't be a problem.

What I like most about all of these films is their most obvious quality. That is their fairly quiet and reflective demeanor and the ability to use this to approach interesting social problems of the time in a nuanced way. When the topic of Japan's role in the Second World War comes up in An Autumn Afternoon, the conversation is relatively uncontroversial and appropriately dispassionate such that after the protagonist (who was a captain of a destroyer) says "but I think it's good we lost", not much else needs to be discussed. This reserved and realist approach seems to me to provide an insightful framework for exploring social topics like the changing expectations of women in society and the treatment of the elderly by their children and Ozu coordinates this well in a way that is neither too obscure nor overt.

These films are so expressive of certain human qualities that they almost don't warrant too close an examination into their more technical attributes. I feel as if they are best enjoyed when not over analysed but simply looked at and appreciated for the moments they strike in you a strong sense of relatability or profundity.
