W003: Akira Kurosawa

Week starting: 2020-10-05

Rashomon (1950)

I like how much this film achieves with what can feel like so little. It has a small cast, but one you get to know over the course of the film, and a small plot that deepens as it goes on. It also makes good use of music to highlight things happening on screen in a way that resembles a lot of silent era films. There's a lot to think about here and get out of this film if you're willing to go along with it.

Seven Samurai (1954)

Seven Samurai epitomises what I like best epic period films. It does a really great job of providing an interesting insight (regardless of how inaccurate or over romantisised) into the way people used to live their lives.

Takes its time (it has a long runtime) but it is still enjoyable throughout. It is fun to watch the recruitment of each of the samurai and the events which transpire to establish the group.

Yojimbo (1961)

Is it necessarily better than A Fistful of Dollars? Probably. What is fascinating is that not only was the overarching plot stolen, but also several smaller details were taken as well (coffin maker, reunited family, plot-significant arson).

Maybe such plagarism is a testament to certain universal qualities in storytelling, or at least filmmaking. It's amazing how well the same story can work in two totally different parts of the world and periods of history.

Notes and Conclusions

Something that is immediately apparent in these films is the influence they have had on other films. Aside from the obvious impact Yojimbo and Seven Samurai have had on Westerns and other action thrillers, Rashomon is also a good early example of the 'unreliable narrator' on the screen, and it's success, I think, must have something to do with how on-the-the-pulse this film is regarding a confusing component of human experience.

If you enjoy explorations on moral ambiguity as much as I do then you will likely enjoy these films as much as I did.

As with Costa-Gavras and Sergio Leone (and many other directors still), Kurosawa employs the "90% casting" rule by re-recruiting Toshiro Mifune, Takashi Shimura and Daisuke Kat√¥ as characters in these films. Not much to say this week. The films stand for themselves and are each worth watching.
