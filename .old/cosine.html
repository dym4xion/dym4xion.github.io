Computer Operating Systems and Intermediate Networking

Week 1

The first lecture of the unit focused on both: the content overview of the unit; and recounted concepts studied last year, surrounding the nature and characteristics of operating systems. This included briefly looking at: OS structure within a computer; CPU organisation; registers; assembly language; user and kernal modes; interrupts; and the position of interrupt handlers within hardware and software.

The practical tutorial focused on: how to maintain lab books; setting up and running a program in Netbeans; and running java programs from the command line, using args.

It may help to think of this unit as a hybrid of the NETFUN and CAR units from last year.

Week 2

In the lecture this week we looked at Concurrency and Threads. We looked at the scope of concurrent programming and the difference between true parallelism and multi-tasking. As well as this, we looked at some of the intricacies of concurrent programming such as: Non-determinism, interference, and race conditions. And we began to look at how concurrent programming issues can be prevented with critical sections of code and mutual exclusions.

In the tutorial this week we looked at Objects and Fields in Java. Much in the same way as we did in this week's ADPROC tutorial, we looked at class creation and writing methods for classes as well as static and instance variables. As I tried to maintain my knowledge of Java over the Summer, the work here was not too difficult. It's important to remember the significance and the implications of static variables.

Week 3

In this week's lecture and practical session, we looked at the uses and implications of multi-threaded programming, to explore ideas crucial to understanding parallelism and multi-tasking. We also looked at the ramifications of running multiple processes at the same time and how they can be resolved via Mutual Exclusion.

Insightful reading topics could include:

  • Peterson's Algorithm
  • Semaphores

In the lab session we investigated threads and race conditions.

Week 4

In the lecture this week, we looked at synchronisation and deadlock. In general synchronisation, threads often wait for a condition to be satesfied by another thread to continue operating. This can lead to a resource deadlock. Deadlocks can be anticipated through modelling with 'resource allocation graphs'.

We also looked at how deadlocks can be detected, recovered from, and altogether avoided.
